# Facilitation Methods

Detailed facilitation scripts for Design Thinking workshops and sessions.

---

## Method 1: 90-Minute Remote Ideation Session

**Purpose**: Generate a large volume of ideas against 1–3 HMW questions and converge to 3 concepts for prototyping.
**Team size**: 4–8 participants
**Tools needed**: Miro, FigJam, or similar; video conferencing; timer

### Pre-session (facilitator prep — 30 min before)
- Set up digital board with:
  - HMW questions prominently displayed (large text)
  - One sticky note area per participant, color-coded
  - A separate "parking lot" area for ideas that don't fit the HMW
  - Convergence space (2×2 matrix or dot voting area)
- Share the POV statement and HMW questions with participants 24 hours before
- Brief participants: "Come ready to generate ideas, not to evaluate them"

### Agenda

**[0:00 – 0:10] Check-in and warm-up**

Read aloud:
> "Before we generate ideas, we're going to do a quick warm-up to get into creative mode. I'm going to give you 60 seconds to write down as many uses as you can think of for a paperclip. Any use counts, no matter how absurd. Go."

Timer: 60 seconds.

Then: go around the group, each person shares their most unusual idea. Applaud and build on them — "yes, and" energy.

Why: This activates divergent thinking and signals that wild ideas are welcome.

**[0:10 – 0:15] Frame the session**

Read aloud:
> "Today we're ideating against [HMW question(s)]. Our goal is [number] ideas in the next 20 minutes. Remember the rules: defer judgment, build on each other's ideas, quantity over quality, and go for wild ones. We'll evaluate after — not during."

Display the HMW question(s) where everyone can see them throughout.

**[0:15 – 0:25] Round 1: Silent brainstorm**

Timer: 10 minutes, silent.

Instructions:
> "Generate as many ideas as you can on your own, one per sticky note. Write fast. Don't edit yourself. If you get stuck, look at the HMW question again and start with 'what if we...'"

Facilitator: Stay silent. Let the discomfort of silence do its work.

**[0:25 – 0:35] Round 2: Build and share**

Go around the group. Each person briefly describes 2–3 ideas they generated. Others can react with "yes, and..." and add new notes as the conversation sparks new directions. Keep moving — this is not a discussion, it is an inspiration round.

**[0:35 – 0:50] Round 3: Stimulus and push**

Choose one of the following to break through the obvious ideas:

Option A — Worst Possible Idea:
> "Now I want you to come up with the worst possible solution to this HMW. The most impractical, offensive, or absurd idea you can think of. Go."
Timer: 5 minutes. Then share. Ask: "Is there anything hidden inside these terrible ideas that could be useful?"

Option B — Forced analogy:
> "Think about how [unrelated domain: airlines, kindergartens, restaurants, hospitals] solve a similar problem. What would they do? Steal the best idea and apply it here."
Timer: 5 minutes.

Option C — Role-play:
> "How would [Amazon / Apple / a 5-year-old / a 95-year-old / your biggest competitor] solve this? Generate 5 ideas from their perspective."
Timer: 5 minutes.

**[0:50 – 1:05] Convergence: Dot voting**

Instructions:
> "Everyone gets [3–5] dots. Vote silently for the ideas you think are most worth exploring. You can put multiple votes on one idea if you feel strongly. Do not campaign for your ideas — just vote."

Timer: 5 minutes silent voting.

Reveal and group: identify the 3–5 most-voted ideas. Spend 10 minutes discussing them briefly — not to decide, but to understand what each idea is really proposing.

**[1:05 – 1:15] Select concepts for prototyping**

Facilitate a brief structured decision:

For each top idea, ask:
1. "What problem from our POV does this solve?"
2. "What assumption does it make that we'd need to test?"
3. "On a scale of 1–5, how different is this from what already exists?"

Select the top 3 concepts — ideally representing a range (safe, interesting, wild).
Assign a concept owner to each who will lead prototyping.

**[1:15 – 1:20] Debrief and next steps**

- Name the 3 concepts selected
- Confirm concept owners
- Confirm prototype format and deadline
- Schedule test sessions

## Method 2: Research Synthesis Workshop (Affinity Diagramming)

**Purpose**: Transform raw research notes into patterns, insights, and a POV statement.
**Team size**: 3–6 (all should have participated in or reviewed the research)
**Duration**: 2–3 hours
**Tools**: Physical sticky notes + wall, or Miro/FigJam for remote

### Pre-session (facilitator prep)
- Participants must have read research notes or attended sessions
- Prepare: one sticky note per raw observation from research (behavioral, not interpreted)
- If remote: pre-populate digital notes from each session, one note = one observation

### Agenda

**[0:00 – 0:10] Ground rules**

Read aloud:
> "Our job today is to let patterns emerge from the data — not to confirm what we already think. Write observations, not interpretations. 'She called her daughter three times before attempting the form' is an observation. 'She was confused' is an interpretation. We want observations."

**[0:10 – 0:40] Silent clustering**

Timer: 30 minutes, no talking.

Instructions:
> "Move the notes into groups that feel related. If a note could go in two groups, duplicate it. If a note doesn't fit anywhere, start a new cluster. Do not pre-define category names — let them emerge."

Facilitator: Move around, observe, do not direct. If two people move the same note in opposite directions, that tension is data — leave both and note it.

**[0:40 – 1:00] Name the clusters**

As a group, name each cluster using a descriptive phrase — not a one-word label.

Good cluster names:
- "Users create workarounds when official options fail them"
- "Anxiety spikes at moments of uncertainty, not moments of actual failure"
- "The confirmation step is more important emotionally than practically"

Not: "Workarounds" / "Anxiety" / "Confirmation"

The name should contain a verb and make a claim. If you cannot write a claim, the cluster is not coherent yet — split or recombine.

**[1:00 – 1:20] Identify tensions and the sharpest insight**

Ask the group:
- "Which two clusters seem to contradict each other? What does that tell us?"
- "Which finding surprised us most? Which did we not expect?"
- "If we could only tell one thing to the team, what would we say?"

The answer to the last question is the seed of your POV insight.

**[1:20 – 1:50] Draft POV statements**

Each person independently drafts a POV statement using:
`[User] needs [X] because [Y]`

Timer: 10 minutes, individual work.

Share and compare. Look for:
- Where do the drafts agree? (High confidence insights)
- Where do they diverge? (Areas of interpretive uncertainty — may need more research)
- Which "because" is most non-obvious and most grounded in the data?

Refine to 1–3 POV statements as a group.

**[1:50 – 2:00] Generate initial HMW questions**

From the POV statements, each person generates 3 HMW questions in 5 minutes.
Share. Group them.
Select 3–5 priority HMW questions for the next ideation session.

## Method 3: Prototype Critique Session

**Purpose**: Evaluate a prototype (or competing prototypes) before user testing.
**Team size**: 3–8
**Duration**: 45–60 minutes
**Timing**: After prototypes are built, before external user testing

### Ground rules (read aloud)

> "We are here to make the prototype better, not to defend it. Feedback should be grounded in the user and the problem statement, not personal preference. Use the POV statement as the filter: does this design solve the problem we defined for the user we defined?"

Post the POV statement visibly throughout the session.

### Agenda

**[0:00 – 0:10] Present the prototype**

The prototype owner presents for 5–10 minutes, covering:
1. The HMW question this prototype addresses
2. The key assumption it is designed to test
3. What to pay attention to

Rule: No feedback yet. Listen only.

**[0:10 – 0:30] Individual silent review (Rose, Bud, Thorn)**

Each reviewer notes independently:
- **Rose**: What is working — specific moments, decisions, elements that solve the problem
- **Bud**: What has potential but needs development — ideas the design hints at but doesn't deliver
- **Thorn**: What is not working — moments of friction, confusion, or mismatch with the POV

Timer: 15 minutes, no discussion.

**[0:30 – 0:50] Structured share-out**

Facilitator goes around the room in order: all Thorns first, then Buds, then Roses.

Why this order: Ending on Roses reduces defensiveness and leaves the creator with a clear sense of what is worth keeping.

Rules for feedback givers:
- Root feedback in the user: "A user who [user context] would struggle here because..."
- Be specific: reference a specific moment or element, not the whole design
- Avoid prescribing solutions: name the problem, not the fix

Rules for prototype owner:
- Take notes, do not defend
- You may ask clarifying questions: "Can you tell me more about where you saw that problem?"
- You may not explain your intention: "Well, what I meant was..." — if it needs explaining, it needs redesigning

**[0:50 – 1:00] Prioritize and plan**

Facilitator asks:
1. "What are the 1–2 changes that would most improve the chance of this prototype succeeding in testing?"
2. "What assumptions does this prototype make that we are most uncertain about?"
3. "Are we ready to test with users, or does this need another round first?"

Document: agreed changes, owner, and timeline.

## Method 4: 3-Day Innovation Workshop

**Purpose**: Move a team from a problem brief through to testable concepts in 3 focused days.
**Team size**: 6–12 participants (cross-functional: design, research, product, business, and ideally a customer advocate)
**Facilitator**: Dedicated; not a participant
**Pre-work required**: Existing research or a plan to gather it before Day 1

---

### Day 1: Empathize + Define (7 hours)

**Goal**: Shared understanding of the user and a clear problem statement

**[9:00 – 9:30] Orientation**
- Why we are here and what we will produce by Day 3
- Rules: phones away during activities, everyone speaks, no hierarchy in this room
- Schedule overview

**[9:30 – 11:00] Research immersion**

Option A (if research exists):
- Share research: each team presents findings from interviews, observations, data
- Participants review materials individually and write observations (15 min silent)
- Facilitator prompts: "What surprised you? What made you uncomfortable? What contradicted what you believed?"

Option B (live research, if no prior research exists):
- Run 3–4 live problem interviews with recruited participants
- Full team observes (remote or in separate room)
- Debrief immediately after each: "What did we hear? What does it tell us?"

**[11:00 – 12:30] Affinity diagramming**

Follow the Research Synthesis Workshop method (Method 2 above), compressed:
- 30 min silent clustering
- 20 min cluster naming
- 20 min insight identification

Output: Named clusters and the 2–3 sharpest insights

**[12:30 – 13:30] Lunch break**

**[13:30 – 14:30] POV statement development**

- Individual POV draft: 10 min
- Share and compare: 20 min
- Refine to 1–2 POVs as a group: 30 min

**[14:30 – 15:30] HMW question generation**

- Individual generation against the finalized POV: 10 min (3 HMW per person)
- Share all: 20 min
- Group and prioritize: 30 min
- Select 3–5 HMW questions for Day 2 ideation

**[15:30 – 15:45] Break**

**[15:45 – 16:30] Day 1 debrief**
- Review outputs: POV statement(s) and prioritized HMW questions
- Confirm: do these feel right? Is anything missing?
- Preview Day 2: come ready to generate, not evaluate

---

### Day 2: Ideate + Prototype Concept (7 hours)

**Goal**: 3 concept directions selected and rough-sketched for prototyping

**[9:00 – 9:15] Warm-up**
30-uses-for-a-brick exercise (or equivalent). Keep it fast.

**[9:15 – 11:15] Ideation**

Run the 90-Minute Remote Ideation method adapted for in-person:
- Round 1: Silent brainstorm (15 min)
- Round 2: Share and build (20 min)
- Round 3: Stimulus round — Worst Possible Idea (15 min)
- Dot voting and convergence (30 min)
- Concept selection (15 min): top 3 concepts for prototyping

**[11:15 – 12:30] Concept development**

Teams of 2–3 people take one concept each. In 75 minutes:
- Name the concept
- Write a one-sentence description: "This concept helps [user] [do X] by [key mechanism]"
- Identify the riskiest assumption this concept makes
- Sketch the key moment: the one interaction or experience that makes or breaks this concept

**[12:30 – 13:30] Lunch**

**[13:30 – 14:00] Concept gallery**

Post all three concept sketches on the wall. Participants do a gallery walk, silently reading and writing reaction notes.

**[14:00 – 15:30] Prototype planning**

For each concept, define:
- What is the riskiest assumption? (This is what the prototype must test)
- What type of prototype answers this question at lowest cost?
- What does the participant see, hear, and do in the test?
- What will we learn if it works? What will we learn if it fails?

**[15:30 – 15:45] Break**

**[15:45 – 17:00] Prototype build**

Teams build rough prototypes: paper, storyboard, or simple digital sketch.
Goal: something testable tomorrow, not something beautiful.

---

### Day 3: Prototype + Test (6 hours)

**Goal**: Each concept tested with 2–3 real users, findings synthesized, direction selected

**[9:00 – 9:45] Prototype finalization**

Teams spend 45 minutes completing prototypes and preparing test scripts using Template 5.

**[9:45 – 12:00] Testing**

3 rounds of testing, 30–40 min per participant:
- At least 2 users per concept (ideally 3)
- Full team observes each session
- One facilitator per session; others take notes

**[12:00 – 13:00] Lunch + individual synthesis**

Each observer writes behavioral observations (not interpretations) during lunch.

**[13:00 – 14:00] Synthesis**

Full team synthesizes findings using Template 6 (Test Findings Synthesis Table):
- What did users do in each concept?
- What failed? What worked?
- What surprised us?

**[14:00 – 15:00] Direction decision**

Facilitated structured decision:
1. For each concept: summarize findings in 2–3 sentences (behavioral evidence only)
2. Vote: which concept has the most evidence of desirability + feasibility?
3. Discuss: which concept has the most valuable untested potential?
4. Decide: proceed with one concept, hybrid, or back to ideation?

**[15:00 – 15:30] Next steps**

Document:
- Selected direction and why
- Assumptions that still need testing
- Next prototype type and fidelity
- Owner and timeline for next iteration
- Definition of success for the next test round

## Facilitation Principles

### Creating psychological safety for ideation

The quality of ideation depends entirely on participants feeling safe to generate wild ideas.

- **Separate roles**: The person who generated an idea should not be its sole defender. Ideas are not identities.
- **Silence beats pressure**: When brainstorming stalls, add time — do not ask people to share more. The silence is often productive.
- **Model the behavior**: If you want wild ideas, generate a wild idea yourself first.
- **Celebrate the unexpected**: When someone generates something genuinely unusual, make a moment of it.

### Managing authority in the room

When senior leaders or executives are present in ideation:

- Ask them to generate ideas silently alongside everyone else before sharing
- Run dot voting before discussion — once preference is visible in votes, leaders' opinions are one data point, not the deciding factor
- If a leader signals preference during generation, pause: "Let's hold judgment until we've had a chance to generate all our ideas — then we'll evaluate together."

### When a session stalls

Signs of stall and responses:

| Signal | Response |
|---|---|
| Silence that feels stuck, not productive | Switch methods — move from brainstorm to Worst Possible Idea |
| Ideas are all the same | Force analogy — "How would [unrelated domain] solve this?" |
| One person is dominating | Go around the room in a strict order, 1 idea per person |
| The group is evaluating during generation | Stop: "Let's put all judgment on hold. We're generating, not deciding." |
| Energy is low | 5-minute break. Come back with a new stimulus question. |
| Group wants to return to a solution they love | Acknowledge it: "That's on the board. Now let's also explore what else might work." |

### Capturing outputs

Always capture:
- Photos of physical walls/boards at the end of each session
- The shortlisted concepts with rationale
- The deprioritized ideas — these are valuable inputs for future work
- The exact wording of the final POV statement(s) and HMW questions

Designate one person as the "output owner" before the session starts. Their job is documentation, not facilitation.
